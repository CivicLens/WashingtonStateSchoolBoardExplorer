Public Content
As part of the 2022-2023 City Council Goals & Priorities, the Pullman City Council on October 24, 2023, after
discussions at the City Council Meetings on September 12, 2023 and October 10, 2023, passed
RESOLUTION R-49-23:A RESOLUTION CREATING A CODE OF CONDUCT FOR ELECTED OFFICIALS,
BOARDS, COMMITTEES, COMMISSIONS, STAFF, VOLUNTEERS, AND THE COMMUNITY FOR
TREATING ONE ANOTHER AND TO WORK TOGETHER FOR THE COMMON GOOD OF THE
COMMUNITY AND OTHER MATTERS RELATED THERETO.
The Code of Conduct for elected officials, boards, committees, commissions, city staff, volunteers, and the
community at large outlines the expected practices for the City of Pullman and the community.
The presentation will review the Code of Conduct and answer any questions.

Recommended Action :

By motion, adopt the North Arterial Technical
Memorandum and providing for other matters properly
related thereto.

Public Content
In the early 1970s the Washington State Highway Commission, now known as the
Washington State Department of Transportation (WSDOT) acquired right-of-way (r/w)
and access control for a North Bypass, designated as State Route (SR) 276. This
route was envisioned as a divided limited access highway,proposed primarily to
relieve congestion in the downtown business district where two state highways, SR
27 and SR 270, intersect. This route begins at U.S. Route 195, north of Pullman, and
terminates at SR 270, east of Pullman. When WSDOT purchased the right of way,
they also purchased these limited access rights. The purchase was done with Federal
and State motor vehicle funds; therefore, the r/w can only be used for road and street
purposes.

In 2016, Senate Bill 6607 was introduced in the Washington State Legislature to
decommission the unbuilt highway by removing its designation from the Revised
Code of Washington, thereby ending the states plan to one day construct the North
Bypass. In addition, the portion of r/w between Airport Road and SR 270, was
modified and sold to the Pullman Moscow Regional Airport (PMRA) to facilitate their
runway expansion project.
Later in 2016, WSDOT contacted both the City and Whitman County, indicating that
they would like to transfer the r/w to the City/County at no cost, with the caveat that
the r/w must be used for road and street purposes only. At that point, the City and
County agreed that the best course of action was to first hire a Consultant to study
and determine what a City/County arterial street (North Arterial) would look like within
this corridor and determine what else should be considered if the r/w was to be
acquired. At their November 22, 2016 meeting, City Council authorized City staff to
take the lead on this effort by authorizing an Engineering Services Agreement with
Parametrix, Inc. to perform the engineering study. The final draft of that study was
presented to City Council on November 28, 2023 in the form of a North Arterial
Technical Memorandum. A presentation was also made to Whitman County on
November 20th, 2023.
No changes were requested to the study by either the City Council or by Whitman
County at said meetings. Therefore, the study is now being brought to Council for
formal adoption.
The study determined that the right-of-way is acceptable for a City/County arterial
street and recommends that both the City and County move forward with acquiring
the right-of-way from WSDOT. Prior to right-of-way acquisition, the study also
recommends that WSDOT releases the existing limited access rights, as they are no
longer applicable to a City/County street. The study identified some areas where
additional r/w and construction easements will be necessary. These areas shall be
preserved for the purpose of the eventual North Arterial construction.
Staff recommends that Council adopt the North Arterial Technical Memorandum and
also authorize staff to begin working with WSDOT to acquire the right-of-way. Any
subsequent right-of-way agreements with WSDOT will be brought to Council for their
acceptance.

1. WaterSMART Water & Energy Efficiency Grant
Application FY2024 for Citywide Upgrade to Advanced
Metering Infrastructure

Public Content
Through WaterSMART (Sustain and Manage Americas Resources for Tomorrow),
the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation provides financial assistance for projects that
conserve and use water more efficiently, implement renewable energy, investigate
and develop water marketing strategies, mitigate risk of future water conflict, and
accomplish other benefits that contribute to sustainability in the western United
States. WaterSMART Water and Energy Efficiency Grants focus on projects that
result in quantifiable and sustained water savings, and replacing existing meters with
Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI) technology is one of the eligible water-saving
project types.

The City has been planning a citywide upgrade to AMI technology, and the project is
in the Capital Improvement Program (CIP), planned to be partially grant-funded.
Advanced Metering Infrastructure provides a fully interconnected system giving realtime water usage data to staff and customers, as opposed to once-a-month readings.
This will lead to more accurate water billing and significant water conservation
through timely leak detection as well as customer awareness. This is an essential
part of the City's efforts to stabilize the regions precious water supply from the
Grande Ronde Aquifer, and will also reduce greenhouse gas emissions from vehicle
miles driven for meter reads and by increasing energy efficiency of the water system.
The Notice of Funding Opportunity for the latest cycle of the WaterSMART grant was
posted on November 14, 2023, and the closing date for Fiscal Year 2024 applications
is February 22, 2024. The grant requires a 50% cost share of the total project cost.
Selections are expected to be announced this summer, followed by an anticipated
award date of December 31, 2024. The Bureau of Reclamation expects to choose
approximately 40-50 projects for grant awards for this application period. The grant
has different funding levels, with a maximum award ceiling of $5,000,000 per
agreement for projects that may take up to 3 years to complete.
At the time of the City's last grant application in July 2022, the total project cost was
estimated at $3.5 million dollars. Staff feels that this opportunity is worth pursuing to
leverage existing CIP funds and share half of this planned projects cost with federal
funds. Grant funding will allow the City to hire a contractor to install the devices to
complete the project in a much timelier manner than using in-house resources that
are experiencing capacity issues.
Staff is currently planning to pursue this grant opportunity, and proposes to bring forth
the draft application to City Council for authorization on the Consent Agenda at their
February 13thCouncil meeting, ahead of the grant submission deadline of February 22
ndif so directed. Staff has unsuccessfully applied for this grant twice before, but
received valuable feedback and reinforced the project with lots of attention and work
being contributed by Public Works Director Sean Wells, Maintenance & Operations
Supervisor Art Garro, Deputy Public Works Director Clayton Forsmann, Development
Engineer Scott Palmer, and Grants & Special Projects Manager Valerie Weaver.
Staff is requesting input and direction from City Council regarding staffs proposed
plan to apply for the WaterSMART Water and Energy Efficiency (FY 2024) grant.
